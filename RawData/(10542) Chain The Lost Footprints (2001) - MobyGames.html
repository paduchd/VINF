Description official description

In Chain: The Lost Footprints the player takes the role of Takeshi Shinonome, a private investigator. One day he accidentally meets a girl whose sister studied with him together in the school. She suspects her husband is unfaithful to her, and asks Takeshi to investigate the affair. What starts as a simple family investigation soon turns into a ghastly murder case involving a mysterious lethal drug that makes people insane.

The game is a Japanese-style adult adventure. The player is given several options at each screen (such as "think", "look", or "talk"); by selecting them repeatedly, the player triggers the advancement of the plot. There is no inventory or puzzles of any kind in the game, as well as no branching storyline: the game proceeds the exact same way regardless of the player's input.

Probably the most exciting bishoujo game yet released in the US. Recommended with reservations.

The Good
Let me start by saying that I'm a sucker for film noir. I saw The Big Sleep when I was a young kid, and I immediately fell in love with the drama and sense of style. Every private detective in every movie, book, and video game since has played it like Bogart, still wearing trench coats and fedoras decades after they have gone out of fashion, and speaking in monologues that would sound like utter kitsch anywhere else. And, well, it just never gets old for me. I love it.

Chain is an incredibly thrilling game, but it helps if you go into it knowing that it isn't really a "game" in the sense that you actually influence it. There is token "interactivity" inasmuch as you can look at what you want, and talk to who you want, and this allows you to examine your surroundings at your own pace. But, the game won't continue until you have picked every option. There are also points where you must make a decision from a list, but the plot branches, if there are any, must be minor; the first time I played through the game, I had unlocked every CG and scene. Perhaps the game ends if you make the incorrect decision, but having seen everything already I didn't really care to find out. So Chain is less a game that you play, and more a computer movie that you experience.

And if that's all it is, then it might be the best ever. Somewhat reminiscent of Rise of the Dragon (private detective gets to bottom of sinister drug ring and corporate corruption), the story is paced very well and full of suspense. Each character is memorable and interesting, and you will grow to like them all. The climax had me simply giddy with anticipation. I can honestly think of very few games that have ever had me more excited.

Chain's graphics are some of the best ever featured in a bishoujo game. The artists here really went all-out to give the game an extremely cinematic feel. In all, the game features over 100 CGs, all drawn to perfection. The stills are easily as good as most any animated film. The character designs also deserve recognition; the game's women are each unique and beautiful. In an animation industry that releases countless TV series, films, OVAs, manga, and games every year, it is extremely difficult for character designers to come up with something their audience hasn't seen before. There are over 50 bishoujo game titles alone released every month in Japan. Playing Chain, I was impressed time and again by the artists' effort to go against the norm.

Also worth mentioning is the fact that I quite enjoyed the game's soundtrack. I usually play bishoujo games with the sounds off, because when you cram a half hour of music into a game that takes several hours to play (Chain will probably take you about 6+ hours), it naturally becomes quite repetitive. I quite enjoyed Chain's mix of jazz and lounge music, however. I have also been told that the game's voice acting is quite good, but I play bishoujo games with the voices off because I don't want to wait for the voice actors to finish talking while I advance through the text.

The Bad
I love Chain for what it is; not so much a game as computer audio/visual entertainment. Still, playing this game, you can't help but think that it would be just a rung below such all-time classics as the Gabriel Knight series, had it only been done as a traditional adventure game. The gameplay mechanic in Chain is both a blessing and a curse.

Additionally, the game's replay value is low. You will probably want to experience the story again in the future, but you will not see anything different. Once you have finished it, you've seen all that it has to offer. One might like to have seen the ability to end up with a different girl at the story's conclusion, for instance.

Finally, the thing that links all bishoujo games into a common genre - graphic hand-drawn depictions of sex - is exactly what will limit its audience to a small niche group of anime fans and adventure gamers. There are women who play bishoujo games, certainly, but countless other women who love adventure games won't touch Chain even though the story is right up the alley of any mystery fan. (Not to say that there aren't men who wouldn't play a bishoujo game either, of course.) And our boy, Takeshi... well, let's just say he is a bit of a gigolo. You can just about count on the fact that every woman he meets will eventually be his bedmate. Chain actually has a rather touching romantic subplot, but it's cheapened a little by the fact that Takeshi really gets around.

On the up side, this fact isn't without its benefits. Hey, all of the women in the game are beautiful, and you will get to see them all naked. That isn't all bad. The sex scenes are drawn tastefully for the most part, and are mainly consensual. Be aware, however, that Chain is not shy about violence. There are a few scenes of non-consensual sex, and a bit of blood is spilled (nothing that I couldn't handle though, and I can get grossed out easily).

The Bottom Line
Chain challenges the definition of "computer game". It's barely interactive; the story will go one way, whether you like it or not. It isn't challenging. You can't "win" or "lose" it, you can only let it take you along for the ride. It might have worked as a movie. Yet, the game is as long as three movies if you breeze through at a brisk reading pace like I did; it might even be twice that length if you listened to all of the recorded dialogue (I might, next time I play). So, as a movie it would have to be trimmed significantly.

Nevertheless, if it were made into a movie, I would buy it, even knowing the entire plot already. Chain would be a successful film. If it were a book, it would be a page-turner. Just like a truly great song shines in any arrangement and instrumentation, a great story works in any medium. If you are prepared to accept the fact that you will not be playing a game, but rather experiencing computer entertainment, buy Chain. It's a true winner.

Windows · by Eurythmic (2666) · 2004
