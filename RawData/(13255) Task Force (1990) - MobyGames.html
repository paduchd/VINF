Description

Crime has risen to unprecedented levels. Criminal organizations have banded together - all under the leadership of Hector Vasquez, the mastermind behind it all.

It is up to Joe Baxter and Michael Dvorak, tough cops from New York and Chicago respectively, to eliminate all the goons and henchmen of the mobs until they can go after Vasquez himself - with whom the two have a personal beef they'd love to settle.

The game itself is a side-scrolling shoot-'em-up that can be played with one or two players simultaneously, and is very similar to Narc. In five major US cities, all the enemies must be taken out using different weapons like flame throwers, tazers, uzis, rocket launchers, and grenades.

The masters at work.

The Good
Scott Patterson and Matt Crysdale - when you see these names, you know something good is waiting for you.

Task Force is a wonderful example of their skills: Numerous huge sprites fill the scrolling screen at a steady frame-rate. Even when the going gets tough, the game doesn't slow down. The graphics are nicely drawn and well animated. The sound doesn't excel in variety (plus it contains some recycled samples from Great Western Shootout, which in turn stole samples from Sword of Sodan), and there is a complete lack of music, but it still works well for the game.

Although the game is linear, it was a nice touch to randomize the order in which the cities are selected - that way, the flow of the game varies at least a bit. And the game itself - is just fun.

It's hard to go into detail, because the good thing about this game is that it's just fun. It's not too hard, it's not too easy, it doesn't get boring quickly, you never have to wait long, it's never unfair, it's too much fun in two-player mode, the weapons add a great deal of variety. It's simply a perfect example for a well-tuned action game.

The Bad
Not much. Maybe it sucks to have to play through the entire game all the time, but then, this is not an adventure game, it's an action game. Playing the game is the reward, not making progress.

Although it sometimes does feel weird that any of the five cities is randomly selected as a starting point - sometimes you start in San Francisco (which I feel to be rather difficult), sometimes you start in an easy level. This is unlike normal games where the difficulty level gradually rises.

The Bottom Line
Back in the early 90s, this was one of my favorite games. I used to play it for hours with friends. It's a technical masterpiece, and from a game design perspective, it's a perfect balance between difficulty and playability. You'll always get to play for a long time because your character can take a lot of hits, but you'll still notice how you gradually get better as you continue to practice.

The little "storyline" inbetween adds to the atmosphere, and little details that you gradually discover are the finishing touches. This is an awesome game without any useless baggage attached to it.

Apple IIgs · by EboMike (3080) · 2004

Trivia

The laptop computer sports the number "655321", which is a direct reference to the movie and book "Clockwork Orange" (Alex' prisoner ID number). Graphic artist Matt Crysdale has confirmed that reference and also noted that he tries to hide that number in every project he works on.
