Description official description

Rails Across America is a game of railroad empires in North America, covering the period from 1830 to the near future. It is a real-time strategy game with a "sim" flavor. Rails allows hundreds of tracks with thousands of trains crossing the continent. Players focus on strategic decisions: where to expand, where to compete, and when to call in favors from government officials, union leaders, and other powerful figures.

Reviews

Critics

Average score: 69% (based on 16 ratings)

Average score: 3.8 out of 5 (based on 7 ratings with 1 reviews)

Deep and multi-layered game play. A rare jewel in a sea of cookie-cutter RTS games.

The Good
Rails Across America is a simple game at heart, the central idea being to build connections between cities. Behind this simple concept is some deep and addictive game play, with many factors under players’ control. The number of charts and tables that need to be accessed, and the amount of information that a player needs to reference throughout the game may seem daunting at first, but everything is tied together elegantly and logically. An RTS that does not play like an RTS (i.e., you do not gather resources and send your units into battle), Rails Across America may cause a new player some initial confusion. A half hour after I first started exploring this game, I had one of those “a-ha!” moments wherein I got it – I suddenly understood the game, and the various menus and charts made sense.

Not since LucasArts’ Their Finest Hour has a game taught me so much about a period or aspect of history. I’m not a train enthusiast, but I’ve come away from playing Rails Across America with a wealth of information about the history of North American rail. The AI behaviour of computer players is patterned after the real life behaviour of moguls such as Drew, Vanderbilt, Fisk and Crocker. Some computer players will play aggressively and some defensively, some will seek out alliances with other players, and some will act alone. Multiplayer is implemented flawlessly.

Rails Across America is well supported by its developer, Flying Labs. An internet site offers a chat forum that is actively participated in by many players, as well as patch downloads, technical advice, and FAQs. If you are connected to the internet, Rails Across America will inform you upon starting if an update for the game exists (currently it is at 1.0.0.75)

At first, the olive green map seems a little mundane, but when you play a game with many players, the logic of this colour choice is obvious. Against the olive green, the multi-coloured lines signifying rail connections stands out well. You have the ability to zoom in very close to the terrain and see individual trains, and features specific to each city (e.g., the CN Tower in Toronto or the Sears Tower in Chicago). However, this view is not very useful in gaming, and you will spend most of your time zoomed out as far as possible. Much time is also spent in the Tracks List, managing and making upgrades to your tracks and trains. There are many other screens that present other kinds of information, such as political influences and economic factors. All of these screens need not be accessed, as it is possible to play Rails Across America in different ways. A number of options for game play exist, further tailoring the gaming experience to individual tastes. For example, the ability to include political intrigue in your game can be turned off, or if you would prefer that bankruptcy results in a player being eliminated from the game, this too can be set.

Rails Across America came out in an era when strategy games were in a rut, all following the same pattern of collecting resources, building armies and attacking your enemy (come to think of it, strategy games are still, by and large, in that rut). As such, Rails Across America was and is a breath of fresh air.

The Bad
The music in the game consists solely of a banjo playing “I’ll Be Working On The Railroad”. That’s it. Nothing more. Now, you can switch the music off (and you will, you will). I understand that the tune makes sense for this kind of game. But why stop there? I can’t see that it would have been too much more work to throw in some more tunes. There are many other railroad oriented folk songs out there, so Flying Labs should have put a little more time and effort into this aspect of the game.

Many of the buttons used to access information or make changes are very small and so difficult to use. In addition, greater control over some of the information presented in the tables is sorely needed. If, for instance, you wish to hire managers for all of your tracks, you have to click on each track separately and make the necessary changes; there is no option presented to simply hire managers for every track. If you wish to change one half of your freight tracks to a new diesel engine, you will likely have to select each track individually (there is an option to change all of your freight engines at once, but no option to change just some of them). Since the best controller for this game is a mouse and keyboard, the ability to drag and highlight multiple items at once would have helped immensely.

Relief features are difficult to see on the game map, although they do affect game play (e.g., it takes more money and effort to build across a mountain range than across a prairie).

The Bottom Line
Rails Across America is a breath of fresh air in a stagnant RTS world. A simple concept at heart, it is extremely well designed and offers deep and addictive game play to those who give the game a chance.

Windows · by Les Nessman (265) · 2005
