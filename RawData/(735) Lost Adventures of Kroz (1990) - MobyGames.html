Description

This larger than usual Kroz game (75 levels worth) has the same elements that were present in the previous installments: monsters, puzzles and plenty of levels.

The smiley face dons his whip for the final time

The Good
Kingdom of Kroz is an ASCII-based action/adventure game originally created in 1987, which Scott Miller created for a national gaming competition. His work was distributed by Softdisk, and Miller didn't think it would be a huge success. Afterwards, he started his own software company, Apogee Software, and created more Kroz games, each new one adding new gameplay mechanics and new puzzles for the player to tackle. He also invented the "shareware model" in which people were encouraged to pay a small fee to Miller at his address in Texas, and they could get the rest of the games, along with some goodies. The final game in the series was the Lost Adventures of Kroz, and it is much bigger than any Kroz game to date, containing a whopping seventy-five levels.

As usual, you play the yellow smiley face equipped with a whip that can kill any enemy or terrain surrounding you. You need to use this a lot, as you have to deal with three types of monsters that chase after you, with the blue monster being more dangerous. Their appearances vary depending on what graphic card you have. In my version, the green monsters resemble two Greek letters, switching from Phi to Beta and vice versa. The weapon can be upgraded by collecting white rings that appear occasionally.

You have a sufficient supply of items including gems (health), whips, teleports, and keys to get you started, although these vary depending on the difficulty level you selected before the game begins. You can get more items either out in the open or in chests (depicted as a red square with a yellow C inside). If you have played the previous two Kroz games, you had to deal with walls that try to trap you. They make a return in this game, and you deal with them by using your whip. Between the levels, you can get bonus items in a room almost full with monsters. In these types of levels, you need to dash for the exit in order not to take a great amount of damage.

Two new features have been added in Lost Adventures. One is the diary that you get to read every ten levels as you make your way through a tunnel. Although these take up most of the space on the screen, they are well worth reading. Another important feature is the blue tablet. Collect this and secret passages will open up throughout the level. Finally, Lost Adventures introduces “Sideway” levels, in which Miller encourages you to view them as if your monitor is tilted on its side. As far as I am concerned, these levels force you to go down to the bottom of the screen if you remain stationary.

Even with the ASCII-style graphics, some of the levels look spectacular. In the final level of the game, the last artifact that you collect is stored inside a well-drawn Ankh. The sound effects are the same as those in other Kroz games. The one that plays when you kill the statue that takes your gems is just fantastic.

The save system is also different this time around. Up to three games can be saved, meaning if there is a level you really like, you can reserve a specific slot for it, but just keep saving to the main slot of your choice. The slots can also be used for backup purposes, in case you happen to be slapped with a “Runtime Error” whenever you load a level.

The Bad
I find the “Sideways” levels annoying. As I mentioned, you keep falling down until you get to the bottom edge of the screen. More often than not, though, I ended up in lava and I am forced to use a teleport, warping to places that I didn’t want to be.

The Bottom Line
I enjoyed every Kroz game that Miller put out there. Each one features beautiful graphics and unusual game mechanics. I like how he has catered for low-end systems as well as ones that were released around the time Lost Adventures was released. Sadly, the next game in the series was cancelled because Miller moved onto other projects.

DOS · by Katakis | カタキス (43051) · 2021

Trivia

Cancelled successor

Another 'last Kroz' game was planned for 1991; the Underground Empire of Kroz (note the Zork influence!) never made it to completion.

Source code

You could buy the source code of this game for $950. It's programmed in Turbo Pascal 5.0.
